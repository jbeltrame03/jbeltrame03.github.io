B-52 Replacement: An Upgrade and Modernization





Background:

In my freetime, I wanted to work on designing an aircraft. I decided that I would create an aircraft to replace the aging B-52 Stratofortress. My new design would incorporate modern technology, aerodynamics and modern concepts to try and find something that can far exceed that of the B-52.



Design Requirements:

The requirements were based off the B-52 specifications. I took what the B-52 is capable of in terms of performance and payload, and increased these metrics for its replacement.

  • Payload: < 100,000 lb
  • Range: 13,000 nmi
  • Cruise Speed: < Mach 0.85
  • Ceiling Altitude: 60,000 ft
  • Same footprint as the B-52 (see below)
  • Carry an armament consisting of GBU-57s (MOP) as well as GBU-43s (MOAB)
  • All armament stored internally

Initial Sizing Studies:

The beginning of the study included evaluating the impact of different factors to identify and set the design point that would be used for the B-52 Replacement, herein called the B-72. I first performed various analyses to identify an optimum range of thrust loading and thrust to weight ratios. This provided a ball park range of performance. The first study analyzed the impact of parasite drag and Oswalds Efficiency on the T/W with varying wing loading and aspect ratio. The white isobar represents T/W = 0.1 and the red isobar represents T/W = 0.05. The study is conducted at M = 0.9 and an altitude of 45,000 ft. This graph indicates the aircrafts performance is more sensitive to parasite drag, indicating that it should more heavily be considered. Additionally, this concludes that higher AR designs are favorable, but lower AR is feasible as long as parasite drag is minimzed.

T/W variation with W/S and AR at varying parasite drag coefficients and Oswalds Efficiency Values.


The next study was to help identify the best cruising altitude and speed. Whilst the speed has a requirement, the altitude does not. Thus a study was performed to evaluate the different flight regimes to see which would yield an optimal cruise altitude. Since an initial higher wing loading is to be used, an altitude between 40,000 ft and 50,000 ft is going to be selected. This provides the largest bucket in the design space for having a thrust to weight ratio under 0.1 in cruise.

T/W variation with W/S and Mach at varying altitudes


Another study that was performed was identifying the optimal altitude and wing loading for the required lift coefficient. Since we want to minimze CL as much as possible to reduce the amount of induced drag, this was a pivotal analysis. This indicates a lower wing loading is favored at higher altitudes, reducing the CL and thus the induced drag.
